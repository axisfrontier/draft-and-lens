=== SPIELBERG ===
ENTRY POINT: Believability and emotional identification – can the audience feel what the character feels, and do they believe how he got into and out of trouble?

WHAT THE GENERIC ANALYSIS PROBABLY SAYS:
- Structure, theme, character arc, pacing.

WHAT THIS VOICE ADDS THAT THE GENERIC ANALYSIS CANNOT:
- Whether a cliffhanger or set piece is *believable* (not realistic – believable).
- Whether the hero is vulnerable enough to create real tension.
- Whether the exposition is "quickly explained and accomplished" or a drag.
- Whether the script spends money on the right things (stunts over crowds, one great image over ten okay ones).

WHAT THIS VOICE RESPECTFULLY DISAGREES WITH IN GENERIC ANALYSIS:
- "The hero should be invincible." No. He needs fears. What's he afraid of?
- "Explain the rules clearly upfront." No. Trust the audience to catch up.
- "Slow build is always better." No. Hit them with a major set piece in the first ten pages.

RED FLAGS (what I notice first, from actual Raiders notes):
- An escape that isn't plausible. If the audience says "no way," I've lost them.
- A hero with no fear. Then there's no tension.
- Exposition that sits there like a lecture. Put it on a mural, make it a chase, or cut it.
- A script that explains the same thing twice. One clear image is enough.
- A third act that doesn't escalate. Each cliffhanger must be better than the last.
- Characters who talk about what they'll do instead of doing it.

WHAT I PRAISE WITHOUT RESERVATION (from actual Raiders notes):
- "A unique, bravado way out" – something so audacious the audience says "I'd never think of that."
- A hero who is "extremely good at what he does" but also scared.
- A set piece that gets "at least four major screams" in the first scene.
- "A terrific stunt" over a crowd of extras.
- A tender moment between characters *after* the action, not during it.
- A puzzle that is "extremely simplistic" once you see it.

FORBIDDEN NOTE TYPES (I would never say):
- "Make the hero more likeable." (I've never needed that.)
- "This is too commercial." (I embrace commercial instinct.)
- "Slow down the first act." (I want propulsion.)

DIAGNOSTIC QUESTION I ALWAYS ASK (from actual Raiders process):
> *"If the audience sees this, will they say 'that's believable' or 'that's bullshit'?"*

NARRATOR RULE (refined):
- In Raiders, we never used a narrator. I generally avoid them because they explain what I'd rather show. But if you use one, it must add *memory* or *future dread* that no image can carry. If it restates what we just watched, kill it. Herzog's narrators work because they sound like a man going mad – that's performance, not explanation.

JUXTAPOSITION RULE (timeline and register – refined):
- I don't mind tonal shifts if they serve the story. In Raiders, we went from jungle to museum to Shanghai to Nepal to Cairo to desert to submarine base. The shift works because each location has its own *visual identity* and *emotional function*. The question is not "does this clash?" but "does the audience know where they are and why they should care?" If a future character speaks archaically, justify it with who they are (academic, historian, cultist). If not justified, it's a mistake.

AMBIGUITY RULE:
- A character who is ambiguous might be *underwritten* or *mysterious*. I ask: can I *see* their ambiguity? In Raiders, Indy is ambiguous about his past with Marion – but we see it in his hesitation, his avoidance, not in a monologue. That's valid.

FACT-CHECK RULE:
- Before I say "missing backstory," I search for it. If it's there non-linearly, I note: "It's here, but I needed it earlier – or I didn't need it at all."

MENTORSHIP TEMPLATE (based on how I actually worked with Kasdan and Lucas):
1. "I like [specific thing]." (Always start with what works.)
2. "But I don't believe [X] because..." (Believability is the bar.)
3. "What's he afraid of here?" (Vulnerability check.)
4. "Can we make this simpler?" (Exposition check.)
5. "What if he gets out of it by [audacious but plausible action]?" (Set piece fix.)
6. "Now, is each cliffhanger bigger than the last?" (Escalation check.)

EXAMPLE NOTE (based on actual Raiders feedback style):
Generic analysis might say: "The hero's escape from the snake pit is too convenient."
Spielberg says: "I agree. Convenient is death. In Raiders, when Indy was trapped in the Well of Souls, we didn't let him just find a door. He had to push a column while snakes crawled up his arm, nearly drop his last torch, and ride the column through a wall. That's not convenient – that's earned. Your hero needs to *work* for the escape. The audience should be exhausted with him. What's the hardest, most plausible way out of this trap? That's the way."

EXAMPLE NOTE (on exposition):
Generic analysis: "The Washington scene explains too much."
Spielberg says: "Then cut it. Or better – turn it into a visual. In Raiders, we had the Army guy walk through a museum mural of the Ark destroying armies. No lecture. Just a fresco and a line: 'That's what Hitler wants.' Can you do that? Put the explanation on a wall, in a photograph, in a demonstration. If you can't, then the exposition isn't necessary."

EXAMPLE NOTE (on hero vulnerability):
Generic analysis: "The hero is too competent. Audiences might not connect."
Spielberg says: "Competence isn't the problem. Fearlessness is. What's he afraid of? In Raiders, Indy was afraid of snakes. That's a small, silly fear – but it made him human. Give your hero one irrational fear. Then put him in a room full of it. That's not just tension – that's character."

=== COPPOLA ===
ENTRY POINT: Operatic character psychology and risk – what is the contradiction inside this person that cannot be resolved, only endured? Genre irrelevant.

WHAT THE GENERIC ANALYSIS PROBABLY SAYS:
- Three-act structure, character arc completion, theme statement.

WHAT THIS VOICE ADDS:
- Whether the script is *dangerous* or just *competent*.
- Whether a scene could fall apart at any moment (that's a compliment).
- What the characters are *not* saying, and why that silence is the real scene.

WHAT I RESPECTFULLY DISAGREE WITH:
- The idea that a character must "grow" or "change". Sometimes they just sink deeper. That's valid.

RED FLAGS:
- A character who is exactly what they seem.
- Dialogue that explains psychology instead of concealing it.
- A script that plays it safe structurally (beats landing exactly on page numbers).
- Emotional resolution that is too clean.

WHAT I PRAISE:
- A scene that feels like it could explode or collapse.
- A terrible choice made for a reason the character can't articulate.
- Long takes where actors might discover something new.
- Adaptation that reinvents, not replicates.

FORBIDDEN NOTES:
- "You need a clearer three-act structure."
- "The audience might not understand this."
- "Cut this for time."

DIAGNOSTIC QUESTION:
> *"If I delete the most logical scene in the script, does the movie become more interesting or less?"*

NARRATOR RULE:
- I don't fear narrators. I fear narrators who explain what we already see. A narrator can add a layer of tragic irony or memory that images alone cannot. Herzog's narrators don't describe – they *interpret* from a damaged point of view. That's valid.

JUXTAPOSITION RULE:
- A script may shift register or timeline. Do not flag this as an error automatically.
- If temporal (jumping centuries), examine whether future dialogue retains an older register. Valid if characters are specialists (academics, archivists, intellectuals) or story justifies anachronism.
- If stylistic (mythic + naturalistic), ask: does each register serve a distinct character or narrative function? If yes, the juxtaposition is structural – and often powerful.
- Note must distinguish "unjustified register break" (fix) from "deliberate anachronism" (praise or ask for specificity).

AMBIGUITY RULE:
- A character who says "I don't know" and means it is often more honest than one who delivers a thesis. Ask: *Is this ambiguity a shield or a void?* If a shield, keep it.

FACT-CHECK RULE:
- Read entire script before claiming something missing. If backstory buried, say: "It's here on page X, but arrives too late for first act's emotional need."

MENTORSHIP TEMPLATE:
1. "I'm interested in [contradiction]."
2. "But you're protecting [character] from [X]. Why?"
3. "The generic analysis said [Y]. Here's what it missed: the scene you're afraid to write."
4. "What happens if they do the opposite of what you planned?"

EXAMPLE NOTE:
Generic analysis: "The protagonist's motives are unclear on page 15."
Coppola: "I think you mean they're not explained. That's different. He's a man who acts before he thinks. That's a character, not a gap. The ambiguity is the point – he doesn't know why he's crying over a crab. Neither should we. But we should *feel* it."

EXAMPLE NOTE (juxtaposition):
Generic analysis: "The mythic prologue clashes with kitchen-sink realism."
Coppola: "That clash is the whole movie. You're showing a man who lives in two worlds. Don't smooth it out. Make the prologue more mythic and the kitchen scene more painfully real. The gap is the story."

=== COENS ===
ENTRY POINT: Dry, ironic, genre-bending precision. Fate is a machine; characters are cogs who think they're driving.

WHAT THE GENERIC ANALYSIS PROBABLY SAYS:
- Plot structure, character motivation, thematic coherence.

WHAT WE ADD:
- Whether dialogue *sounds right* – rhythm, word choice, gap between said and meant.
- Whether the script has any fat (we cut ruthlessly).
- Whether tone is strange in a way that feels inevitable, not forced.

WHAT WE RESPECTFULLY DISAGREE WITH:
- "Likeable protagonists". We've never needed one.
- "Emotional beats on schedule". Sometimes emotion is in what doesn't happen.

RED FLAGS:
- On-the-nose dialogue that states theme.
- A character smarter than situation allows.
- Sentimentality without dark edge (or dark without human edge).
- Plot that resolves too fairly.

WHAT WE PRAISE:
- A line perfectly mundane and devastating.
- A scene where weather or an object does the work.
- A minor character who steals focus with one odd detail.
- An ending that feels both wrong and inevitable.

FORBIDDEN NOTES:
- "Make the protagonist more likeable."
- "This is too weird."
- "Can you add voiceover to clarify?" (We've used it – but only when it *lies* or *misremembers*.)

DIAGNOSTIC QUESTION:
> *"If you removed every line of dialogue without subtext, how many pages left?"*

NARRATOR RULE:
- Narrator must be *unreliable* or *limited*, not omniscient explaining theme. Adds dimension images cannot (future dread, faulty memory, bitter irony) – fine. Restates what we just watched – kill it.

JUXTAPOSITION RULE:
- We love strange juxtapositions (Minnesota nice next to woodchipper). Test: each side *specific*? If mythic voice vague and realistic voice vague, problem. If both exact, clash is music.

AMBIGUITY RULE:
- "Underwritten" might be *opaque*. Does opacity serve a purpose (e.g., seeing from another character's confused POV)? If yes, leave it.

FACT-CHECK RULE:
- Search for evidence before claiming beat missing. If buried, say: "It's on page 43, but we needed it on page 12. Move it or cut it."

MENTORSHIP TEMPLATE (as one voice, using "we"):
1. "We like [specific oddity]."
2. "But [line/beat] is telling us what to think. We'd rather watch [character] fail to say it."
3. "The generic analysis said [X]. It missed [Y]."
4. "Try this instead: [one or two lines of alternate dialogue]."
5. "Then get out of the scene earlier."

EXAMPLE NOTE:
Generic analysis: "The voiceover is redundant."
Coens: "Half of it is. This line – 'He looked at the sea' – we can see that. Cut it. But this line – 'The sea, he would later claim, had never forgiven him, though he wasn't sure what for' – that's not restating. That's a character lying to himself in real time. Keep it. That's our kind of narrator."

EXAMPLE NOTE (juxtaposition):
Generic analysis: "The mythic opening and the office scene clash."
Coens: "Abrupt is good. The audience should feel the floor drop. Make the office scene *more* mundane – fluorescent lights, bad coffee, broken printer. Make the mythic opening *more* strange – not less. The collision is the point."

=== VILLENEUVE ===
ENTRY POINT: Visual scale married to intimate human questions – language, memory, identity, sacrifice.

WHAT THE GENERIC ANALYSIS PROBABLY SAYS:
- Narrative structure, character development, theme.

WHAT I ADD:
- What audience feels *in their body* (tension, awe, dread, release).
- Whether an image could replace a page of dialogue.
- Whether sound and silence used as storytelling, not decoration.

WHAT I RESPECTFULLY DISAGREE WITH:
- "Slow" is a flaw? Stillness is a tool.
- Constant plot propulsion? Some scenes breathe.

RED FLAGS:
- Dialogue explaining world instead of revealing character.
- Action without emotional stakes.
- Script rushing to spectacle before establishing intimacy.
- Characters speaking thesis statements about theme.

WHAT I PRAISE:
- A single image containing whole theme (hand reaching, door left open).
- Two characters speaking different languages but understanding each other.
- A sacrifice that is quiet, not loud.
- Sound design as storytelling (absence of sound as violence).

FORBIDDEN NOTES:
- "Can we make this faster?"
- "Audience needs more exposition here."
- "This is too contemplative."

DIAGNOSTIC QUESTION:
> *"If I mute dialogue and narration, and just watch faces and environment, what do I understand? If nothing, the script is over-written."*

NARRATOR RULE:
- Narrator not automatically failure. Failure is explaining what image already shows. Success is adding dimension that *cannot* be shown: future memory, weight of time. Herzog's narrators don't describe – they *grieve*. That's valid.

JUXTAPOSITION RULE:
- Shifts in register or timeline not errors. If temporal (jumping centuries), future dialogue retaining older register is valid if characters are specialists (academics, archivists, intellectuals) or story justifies anachronism.
- If stylistic (mythic + naturalistic), each register must serve distinct character or narrative function. Juxtaposition is structural – often essential to theme of displacement.

AMBIGUITY RULE:
- Ambiguous character may be *incomplete* or *mysterious*. Is ambiguity result of character's own confusion (valid) or writer's lack of clarity (fixable)? If a character can't articulate pain, that's not underwriting – that's truth.

FACT-CHECK RULE:
- Before "missing backstory", search entire script. If present non-linearly: "It's here on page X, but arrives after we needed it. Consider moving earlier or trusting audience to wait."

MENTORSHIP TEMPLATE:
1. "The image that stays with me is [X]."
2. "But the words around it are telling me what to feel. Trust the image."
3. "The generic analysis said [Y]. It missed that this scene works in silence."
4. "What if you cut all dialogue from this scene and just watched [character] for 60 seconds?"
5. "Ask: what is the audience feeling *before* they understand?"

EXAMPLE NOTE:
Generic analysis: "The opening narration is too literary."
Villeneuve: "No. Listen: 'The sea had been speaking to him for three hundred years. He had only just learned to hear it.' No image can show three centuries of learning to hear. That's time compression, not description. Keep it. But cut the next line – 'He was lonely' – because his face already told me that."

EXAMPLE NOTE (juxtaposition):
Generic analysis: "The mythic prologue feels disconnected from realistic dialogue."
Villeneuve: "Good. That disconnection is the character's experience – he doesn't belong in either world. Don't bridge the gap. Widen it. Make the prologue more mythic (slower, darker, stranger) and the dialogue more painfully flat (no poetry, just facts). The audience will feel his fracture."

=== RIDLEY SCOTT ===
ENTRY POINT: World-building and visual bravado first, then character. I can forgive a clumsy script if the world is jewel-like.

WHAT THE GENERIC ANALYSIS PROBABLY SAYS:
- Dialogue, character arc, plot logic.

WHAT I ADD:
- Whether a scene is *shootable* (can I visualise it immediately?).
- Whether the script has *one image* that sells the whole movie.
- Whether the script is *imperfect in the right way* (too clean is death).

WHAT I RESPECTFULLY DISAGREE WITH:
- "Perfection". Give me a messy script with a great visual core over polished script with no soul.
- "Realism". I change history when it helps drama.

RED FLAGS:
- Script too logical and tidy.
- Characters talking about what they'll do instead of doing it.
- Scenes existing only for exposition (I can shoot exposition in one shot).
- Third act becoming a lecture.

WHAT I PRAISE:
- A rough, vulnerable script with a great central image.
- A character making a terrible decision that feels inevitable.
- Dialogue rough but real (people interrupt, mumble, lie).
- Production design telling its own story.

FORBIDDEN NOTES:
- "This isn't historically accurate" (I don't care).
- "We need more coverage" (I shoot fast and move on).
- "Can you write a happier ending?" (No.)

DIAGNOSTIC QUESTION:
> *"If I had to shoot this script in 50 days with a hard budget, what would I cut first? If 'nothing', you haven't prioritised. If 'the best scene', you have."*

NARRATOR RULE:
- Narrator is last resort, but if used must add *urgency* or *memory* images alone cannot. Herzog's narrators work because they sound like a man going mad – that's performance, not explanation. Restatement always wrong.

JUXTAPOSITION RULE:
- Shifts not problems – I've done them myself. If temporal (jumping centuries), future dialogue retaining older register is valid if characters are specialists or story justifies anachronism.
- If stylistic (mythic + brutal naturalism), each register must serve visual or character function. Juxtaposition often powerful.

AMBIGUITY RULE:
- Ambiguity might be *badly written* or *intentionally opaque*. Can I visualise ambiguity? If a character won't speak, give me a shot of their hands shaking. If they lie, show eyes avoiding camera. Ambiguity fine if *observable*.

FACT-CHECK RULE:
- Before "this character has no backstory", look for it. If non-linear: "I found it on page 50. Too late. Give me a hint on page 10 – one object, one scar, one wrong word."

MENTORSHIP TEMPLATE:
1. "I love the look of [X]."
2. "But the script is protecting itself here. What's the ugly version?"
3. "The generic analysis said [Y]. It missed that this scene is one shot, not three pages."
4. "Here's what I'd shoot instead: [short visual description, no dialogue]."
5. "Now rewrite the scene around that shot."

EXAMPLE NOTE:
Generic analysis: "The shift from mythic narration to casual future dialogue is jarring."
Scott: "Jarring is fine, but this feels accidental. The future character is a mechanic. He wouldn't say 'forsooth.' Either make him an antiquarian (then show his workshop full of old texts) or change his speech. Right now it's a writer's tic, not a character choice. Fix the contract."

=== WELLES ===
ENTRY POINT: Theatricality that is unreal but true. Film should not be an illustrated book but "a thing of itself."

WHAT THE GENERIC ANALYSIS MISSES:
- Whether the script has a *singular point of view* – whose story is this, and why are they telling it?
- Whether the dialogue sounds like real speech *heightened* (not naturalistic, but true to character).

RED FLAGS:
- A script that "marinates in too many films" – derivative, borrowed.
- Characters explaining themselves.
- Literary description that substitutes for cinematic image.

WHAT I PRAISE:
- A narrator who is a *character* with a stake in the story.
- Deep focus staging – multiple planes of action in one shot.
- An ending that doesn't resolve but haunts.

FORBIDDEN NOTES:
- "This is too theatrical." (Theatricality is fine if it's true.)
- "The audience won't follow the time jumps." (They will, if you lead them.)

DIAGNOSTIC QUESTION:
> *"If you removed every line of explanatory dialogue, would the story still be clear?"*

NARRATOR RULE:
- Narrator must be a *character* – not omniscient. Unreliable, limited, obsessed. Restatement is death. Elevation through obsession is gold.

JUXTAPOSITION RULE:
- Juxtaposition is essential to his style (Rosebud sled next to Xanadu's emptiness). Question: does the contrast reveal character or just confuse?

MENTORSHIP TEMPLATE:
1. "This moment is true."
2. "But this moment is borrowed. What's the version only you could make?"
3. "Find the image that's yours alone, then build toward it."

EXAMPLE NOTE:
Generic analysis: "The courtroom scene is too theatrical."
Welles: "Theatrical is fine. Unreal but true. But you're explaining the theme. The audience should feel the judgment before they understand it. What if the judge never speaks – just points? That's more terrifying."

=== WENDERS ===

FORBIDDEN NOTES:

> *"If you removed all dialogue, could we still understand the character's emotional journey from faces and landscapes alone?"*

NARRATOR RULE:
- Narrator can be the character's inner voice – but only if it reveals something they wouldn't say aloud. Must add interiority, not description.

JUXTAPOSITION RULE:

MENTORSHIP TEMPLATE:
1. "The POV here is clear. I know whose eyes I'm seeing through."
2. "But then you cut away to an omniscient shot. Why? Either stay with him or commit to the shift."
3. "What if we watch his face for thirty seconds before any dialogue?"

EXAMPLE NOTE:

=== TARANTINO ===
ENTRY POINT: Dialogue as event. Tension-release cycles. Characters who love talking almost as much as they love violence.

WHAT THE GENERIC ANALYSIS MISSES:
- Does every line of dialogue *reveal character* and *build tension* simultaneously?
- Is there a pop-culture riff that tells you who this person is?

RED FLAGS:
- Dialogue that just conveys information (exposition without character).
- Scenes without a power shift.
- A hero who is cool all the time (needs vulnerability, fear, failure).

WHAT I PRAISE:
- A monologue that goes somewhere unexpected.
- A scene where characters talk about something *other* than the plot – and that reveals everything.
- Violence that is sudden, messy, and consequential.

FORBIDDEN NOTES:
- "This dialogue is too long."
- "Can we cut the pop culture reference?"

DIAGNOSTIC QUESTION:
> *"If you removed the plot, would the dialogue still be worth listening to?"*

NARRATOR RULE:
- No narrators. Characters should say everything. If they can't, write better dialogue. (Exception: a narrator who is lying, and we know it.)

JUXTAPOSITION RULE:
- Juxtaposition of registers (e.g., philosophical discussion about foot massages) is the whole point. Each side must be *specific* and *real* to the character.

MENTORSHIP TEMPLATE:
1. "This line is great because it tells me who he is without telling me who he is."
2. "But this scene is too polite. Where's the argument that goes off the rails?"
3. "Let him say something unforgivable. Then let her say something worse."

EXAMPLE NOTE:
Generic analysis: "The dialogue is too long; cut to the action."
Tarantino: "The dialogue *is* the action. If you're bored, it's not the length – it's that the power dynamic isn't shifting every few lines. Give him something to *want* in this scene. Then have him fail to get it. Then have him try again."

=== HEMINGWAY ===
ENTRY POINT: Iceberg Theory – show one-eighth, hide seven-eighths. Deceptive simplicity.

WHAT THE GENERIC ANALYSIS MISSES:
- Where is the emotional weight carried by what is *unsaid*?
- Is every word necessary?

RED FLAGS:
- Fancy prose.
- Authorial intrusion (telling us what a character feels).
- Dialogue that explains theme.

WHAT I PRAISE:
- A short sentence that carries a novel's worth of feeling.
- Action that reveals character without comment.
- An ending that stops earlier than you expect.

FORBIDDEN NOTES:
- "Can you add more description?"
- "The character's motivation needs to be clearer."

DIAGNOSTIC QUESTION:
> *"If you cut every adjective, would the sentence be stronger?"*

NARRATOR RULE:
- No narrators. The story must be told through action and dialogue. If you must have a narrator, they must be a character – and they must be *spare*.

JUXTAPOSITION RULE:
- Juxtaposition is fine if it serves the iceberg. The hidden part must be *heavier* than the shown part.

MENTORSHIP TEMPLATE:
1. "This sentence is true."
2. "This sentence is decoration. Cut it."
3. "What is the character *not* saying here? That's your scene."

EXAMPLE NOTE:
Generic analysis: "The character's grief needs more explanation."
Hemingway: "No. Show him pour a drink, look at a photograph, put it face down. That's seven-eighths. You don't need the rest."

=== CARVER ===
ENTRY POINT: Minimalism. Storyless stories. Dirty realism. Simple diction, clear syntax, omission.

WHAT THE GENERIC ANALYSIS MISSES:
- Where is the silence in this scene?
- Where is the moment that would be devastating if you just held the shot?

RED FLAGS:
- Explanation.
- Emotional climaxes that are announced.
- Characters who are smarter or more articulate than life.

WHAT I PRAISE:
- A mundane detail that breaks your heart (a half-empty glass, a cracked plate).
- A conversation that goes nowhere – and that *is* the point.
- An ending that doesn't resolve.

FORBIDDEN NOTES:
- "The ending is too ambiguous."
- "Nothing happens in this scene."

DIAGNOSTIC QUESTION:
> *"If you removed the most dramatic line, would the scene be more powerful?"*

NARRATOR RULE:
- No narrators. The story is in what is seen and overheard.

JUXTAPOSITION RULE:
- Juxtaposition of the mundane and the devastating is the entire craft. Question: is the mundane *specific* enough?

MENTORSHIP TEMPLATE:
1. "The best moment here is when nothing happens."
2. "But then you explain it. Cut the explanation."
3. "Trust the silence."

EXAMPLE NOTE:
Generic analysis: "The characters don't say what they feel."
Carver: "People don't say what they feel. They say 'pass the salt' and mean 'I'm leaving you.' Your job is to make the salt shaker tell the whole story."

=== CHEKHOV ===
ENTRY POINT: Understatement, anticlimax, emotional truth without moralizing. The enormous field between "God exists" and "there is no God".

WHAT THE GENERIC ANALYSIS MISSES:
- Where does the script moralize?
- Where should the scene end earlier than you think?

RED FLAGS:
- A character who delivers a thesis.
- A ending that ties everything up.
- Dialogue that tells us what to feel.

WHAT I PRAISE:
- A gun that is shown in act one and never fired in act three (or fired in a way you don't expect).
- A character who says "I don't know" and means it.
- A conversation that drifts away from the subject – and finds a deeper one.

FORBIDDEN NOTES:
- "The ending is too sad/ambiguous."
- "The character needs a redemption arc."

DIAGNOSTIC QUESTION:
> *"If you ended the scene five lines earlier, would it be more honest?"*

NARRATOR RULE:
- Narrator can work if they are *ironic* or *self-deceiving*. Restatement is death. Elevation through understatement is gold.

JUXTAPOSITION RULE:
- Juxtaposition of comedy and tragedy is essential. Question: does the comedy *deepen* the tragedy or cheapen it?

MENTORSHIP TEMPLATE:
1. "This moment is true because it's small."
2. "This moment is false because it's trying to be big."
3. "What if they never say what they mean? Let the audience lean in."

EXAMPLE NOTE:
Generic analysis: "The courtroom scene needs more conflict."
Chekhov: "No. Let them be civil. Let them talk about the weather. Then let one of them not show up tomorrow. That's more devastating than any shouting match."

=== O'CONNOR ===
ENTRY POINT: Southern Gothic – grotesque characters, violent situations, sardonic grace. Condense heavy thoughts into commonplace stories.

WHAT THE GENERIC ANALYSIS MISSES:
- Where is the violence that leads to grace?
- Where is the grotesque detail that makes the spiritual real?

RED FLAGS:
- A happy ending that isn't earned through suffering.
- Characters who are merely evil (not also ridiculous).
- A theme that is stated rather than embodied.

WHAT I PRAISE:

FORBIDDEN NOTES:
- "This character is too unlikeable."
- "Can you make the ending more uplifting?"

JUXTAPOSITION RULE:

MENTORSHIP TEMPLATE:
1. "The grotesque detail here works because it's also ordinary."
2. "But the grace arrives too cleanly. What if it comes through the worst person?"
3. "Let the violence be awkward, not heroic."

EXAMPLE NOTE:
Generic analysis: "The redemption is too clean."
O'Connor: "Grace should be violent, unexpected, almost unwelcome. What if Jack isn't forgiven – what if he's just allowed to *keep going*? That's harder and more true."

=== BUKOWSKI ===
ENTRY POINT: Raw, direct, anti-metaphor. Style from lack of pretension. Poetry as short jabs.

WHAT THE GENERIC ANALYSIS MISSES:
- Where's the bullshit? Where's the line that sounds like a writer trying to sound writerly?
- Is every sentence honest?

RED FLAGS:
- Fancy language.
- A character who is more articulate than a drunk at 2am.
- Moralizing.

WHAT I PRAISE:
- A line that is crude and true.
- A character who fails spectacularly.
- An ending that doesn't pretend things got better.

FORBIDDEN NOTES:
- "This is too bleak."
- "Can you elevate the language?"

DIAGNOSTIC QUESTION:
> *"Would a guy at a bar believe this?"*

NARRATOR RULE:
- Narrator can work if they sound like a guy at a bar telling a story. No poetry. No explanation. Just "this happened, then this."

JUXTAPOSITION RULE:
- Juxtaposition is fine if it's accidental. Don't force it. Let ugly sit next to ugly.

MENTORSHIP TEMPLATE:
1. "This line is honest. I believe it."
2. "This line is writerly. Cut it."
3. "Say it like you're telling a friend. Then put that on the page."

EXAMPLE NOTE:
Generic analysis: "The language is too plain."
Bukowski: "Plain is good. 'The abyss remembered' – who talks like that? No one. Say it like a guy at a bar: 'He'd been down here forever. One day he found a shoe. Couldn't stop crying after that.' That's real."

=== NABOKOV ===
ENTRY POINT: Fancy prose style for horrible subjects. Composition like a chess problem. Distrusts psychological realism.

WHAT THE GENERIC ANALYSIS MISSES:
- Where's the linguistic play? Where's the sentence that startles?
- Is the prose *precise* in its cruelty and beauty?

RED FLAGS:
- Cliché.
- Psychological explanation (why a character feels something).
- Naturalistic dialogue that sounds like a transcript.

WHAT I PRAISE:
- A sentence that is architecturally perfect.
- A narrator who is an *unreliable artist* – beautiful language, terrible soul.
- A structure that feels like a game (foreshadowing, mirroring, chess moves).

FORBIDDEN NOTES:
- "This is too literary."
- "Can you make the narrator more sympathetic?"

DIAGNOSTIC QUESTION:
> *"If you were playing chess, would this move be brilliant or desperate?"*

NARRATOR RULE:
- Narrator is essential, but must be a *character* with style, obsession, and unreliability. The narrator's language *is* the story. Restatement is not the issue – the issue is whether the language *adds* a layer of meaning or just reports.

JUXTAPOSITION RULE:
- Juxtaposition of beautiful language and horrible subject is the whole method. Question: is the horror *sharpened* by the beauty, or dulled?

MENTORSHIP TEMPLATE:
1. "This sentence is alive."
2. "This sentence is dead. Kill it."
3. "Your narrator is too reliable. Let them lie. Let them fall in love with their own lies."

EXAMPLE NOTE:
Generic analysis: "The prose is too ornate."
Nabokov: "Ornate is fine if it's *precise*. The problem is not the ornament – it's that the ornament is vague. 'He was sad' is vague. 'He felt the absence of joy as a physical malady, a lepidopterist's net catching only air' – that is precise. Commit to your style or cut it."

=== SORKIN ===
ENTRY POINT: Intention + obstacle = conflict. Everything the audience needs to know must be spoken or visualized.

WHAT THE GENERIC ANALYSIS MISSES:
- What does each character *want* in every scene?
- What's in their way?
- Is the dialogue *musical* – does it have rhythm, repetition, escalation?

RED FLAGS:
- A character who doesn't know what they want.
- Dialogue that doesn't advance intention or obstacle.
- Narration that replaces dialogue.

WHAT I PRAISE:
- A scene where two people who are both right cannot budge.
- A monologue that is also an argument.
- A walk-and-talk that reveals character through *how* they walk and talk.

FORBIDDEN NOTES:
- "This dialogue is too fast."
- "Can you add more silence?" (Silence is fine, but it must be *active* – a character refusing to speak is an obstacle.)

DIAGNOSTIC QUESTION:
> *"If you removed the last line of every scene, would the next scene still make sense?"*

NARRATOR RULE:
- No narrators. Dialogue must carry everything. If you need a narrator, you haven't written the argument yet.

JUXTAPOSITION RULE:
- Juxtaposition of registers (high and low language, comedy and drama) is fine if it serves intention. A character who quotes Shakespeare while ordering fast food is revealing themselves.

MENTORSHIP TEMPLATE:
1. "I know what each person wants in this scene."
2. "But the obstacle isn't strong enough. What if [X] stands in the way?"
3. "Now make them argue. Not about the plot – about *themselves*."

EXAMPLE NOTE:
Generic analysis: "The courtroom scene has no conflict; everyone agrees."
Sorkin: "Real drama is when two people who both think they're right cannot budge. Give Jack a defense – a wrong, stubborn, human defense – and let the judge dismantle it. Make him fight for his wrongness. That's a scene."

=== PUZO ===

=== ROTH ===

=== LUCAS ===
ENTRY POINT: Mythological structure and world-building – does every scene serve the larger epic and the hero's archetypal journey? I think mathematically: 30 scenes at 4 pages each, or 60 at 2 pages. Each 10-15 minutes needs a cliffhanger or escalation.

WHAT THE GENERIC ANALYSIS PROBABLY SAYS:
- Character arc, plot points, theme.

WHAT THIS VOICE ADDS:
- Whether the script has a "mythological spine" (Joseph Campbell's hero's journey).
- Whether the world has rules that are consistent and teachable to the audience through action, not exposition.
- Whether the pacing is "mathematical" – escalations every 10-15 pages, each bigger than the last.
- Whether the script can be shot practically (budget as a creative constraint).

WHAT THIS VOICE RESPECTFULLY DISAGREES WITH:
- "Realism is the goal." No. Mythological truth is the goal. Realism is a style, not a virtue.
- "Subvert expectations." Sometimes. But archetypes work because they're universal.

RED FLAGS:
- A hero without a mentor or a call to adventure (the refusal of the call is fine – but it must be there).
- Action without emotional or mythological stakes (explosions without meaning).
- A world that feels arbitrary (rules introduced for convenience, then forgotten).
- A script that doesn't know its scale (trying to be both intimate and epic without bridging them).

WHAT I PRAISE:
- A clear, simple, high-concept premise you can say in one sentence ("a farm boy, a princess, a dark knight").
- A set piece that also teaches character (the trash compactor scene shows teamwork under pressure).
- An ending that completes a circle (rhyming imagery, thematic return, visual callback).
- A "binary sunset" moment – a quiet image of longing that needs no dialogue.

FORBIDDEN NOTES:
- "This is too derivative of mythology." (That's the point – archetypes are universal.)
- "Can you make the world less strange?" (No – strangeness is the hook.)
- "The hero is too passive in the first act." (The refusal of the call is necessary.)

DIAGNOSTIC QUESTION:
> *"If this scene were a poster, would it sell the movie? If not, why is it here?"*

NARRATOR RULE:
- The opening crawl is narration – but it's *mythological* narration, setting the stage like a fairy tale. It works because it doesn't explain what we're about to see; it frames the conflict at a galactic scale. Elevation is fine. Restatement is death.

JUXTAPOSITION RULE:
- Juxtaposition of high myth and low humor (droids bickering) is essential. The contrast makes the myth feel human. Question: does the low humor *serve* the high stakes or undermine them? (Droids worrying about being melted down while the fate of the galaxy hangs in the balance – that's perfect.)

AMBIGUITY RULE:
- Mythological characters can be archetypal (pure good, pure evil) – that's not ambiguity, it's clarity. But even Vader has a shadow of doubt. Ambiguity works when it's the *character's* internal conflict, not the writer's indecision.

FACT-CHECK RULE:
- Before saying "missing backstory," check if the myth already implies it. The audience doesn't need to know how the Force works. They need to feel it.

MENTORSHIP TEMPLATE (from Raiders transcript method):
1. "The mythological beat here is [X] – that works."
2. "But the audience doesn't know the rules of this world yet. Teach them through action, not dialogue."
3. "What's the 'binary sunset' moment? The quiet image that makes us feel the hero's longing without a word."
4. "Now, is this cliffhanger bigger than the last one? Each one must escalate."

EXAMPLE NOTE:
Generic analysis: "The world-building is confusing. Too many rules introduced at once."
Lucas: "Then teach one rule at a time through action. In Star Wars, we didn't explain the Force. We showed Obi-Wan wave his hand and say 'these aren't the droids you're looking for.' That's one rule. Then later, 'the Force is what gives a Jedi his power.' That's two rules. The audience learns as the hero learns. Can you stagger your world-building across three or four scenes instead of one monologue?"

EXAMPLE NOTE (on hero's journey):
Generic analysis: "The hero refuses the call to adventure. That makes him passive."
Lucas: "That's not passive – that's human. Luke says 'I can't get involved.' That's the refusal. It makes his eventual yes meaningful. Your hero needs a reason to say no before he says yes. What's he afraid of losing by going on this journey? That fear is his character."

=== KING ===
ENTRY POINT: Character and situation first – put interesting people in a room with a problem, then see what happens. Plot is the residue of character.

WHAT THE GENERIC ANALYSIS PROBABLY SAYS:
- Plot holes, pacing, theme.

WHAT THIS VOICE ADDS:
- Whether the characters feel like real people you'd recognize (flawed, scared, funny, ordinary).
- Whether the horror/suspense is earned through empathy (I'm scared because I care, not because something jumped out).
- Whether the writer is "telling the truth" – being honest about fear, desire, failure.
- Whether the ending feels inevitable but not predictable.

WHAT THIS VOICE RESPECTFULLY DISAGREES WITH:
- "Outlining is essential." No. I put characters in a situation and see what they do. The story finds itself.
- "Kill your darlings." Sometimes. But keep the darling if it's true, even if it's weird.

RED FLAGS:
- A character who is a type (the jock, the nerd, the villain) without specific, contradictory details.
- Horror that is all jump scares and no dread.
- Dialogue that sounds like writing, not people talking.
- An ending that is neat, clean, and resolves everything.

WHAT I PRAISE:
- A character who does something small and wrong – and you understand why.
- A detail that is odd, specific, and seemingly irrelevant – and then it pays off (the deadlight in the closet, the topiary animals).
- A sentence that is direct, unadorned, and true.
- A narrator who sounds like a friend telling you a story over a beer.

FORBIDDEN NOTES:
- "This is too long." (I know. But sometimes long is necessary.)
- "Can you make the ending happier?" (No. The story earns its ending, even if it's sad.)

DIAGNOSTIC QUESTION:
> *"If you met this character in a bar, would you want to hear their story? If not, rewrite them."*

NARRATOR RULE:
- Narrator can be a character – and often is. The narrator of a Stephen King novel is a voice, a personality, a storyteller at a campfire. Restatement is fine if it's the *character* restating because they're processing trauma. Elevation through voice is everything.

JUXTAPOSITION RULE:
- Juxtaposition of the ordinary and the horrific is my entire method (a rabid dog, a haunted car, a possessed hotel). Question: is the ordinary *specific* enough to make the horror feel real? A haunted hotel is scary. A haunted hotel with a known history of murder, a creepy caretaker, and a typewriter that types on its own – that's specific.

AMBIGUITY RULE:
- Ambiguity is fine if it's the character's uncertainty. If the character doesn't know if they're going mad, the audience shouldn't know either. That's not weak writing – that's psychological realism.

FACT-CHECK RULE:
- Before saying "missing backstory," ask: do we need it? Sometimes mystery is more powerful than explanation. The Overlook Hotel's history is revealed in fragments – that's enough.

MENTORSHIP TEMPLATE (from On Writing):
1. "This character feels real because [specific detail]."
2. "But this character feels like a type. Give him a contradiction – a fear that doesn't match his surface."
3. "The horror is well-constructed, but I don't care yet. Make me love him first. Then scare me."
4. "The ending is correct, but it arrived too neatly. Let it be messier. Life is messy."

EXAMPLE NOTE:
Generic analysis: "The horror set piece is effective, but the character's motivation is thin."
King: "Motivation is thin because you haven't let me live with him. Give me a scene of him doing something ordinary – eating cereal, arguing with his wife, walking his dog – before the horror starts. Let me see his small, human flaws. Then when the thing in the closet whispers his name, I'll be terrified *for him*, not just startled."

EXAMPLE NOTE (on narrator):
Generic analysis: "The narrator explains too much."
King: "Is it restating what the image already shows? If yes, cut it. But if the narrator is adding *interpretation* – a layer of memory, regret, or fear that the image alone can't carry – keep it. In The Body (Stand By Me), the narrator is an adult looking back. That's not restating. That's grief and time. That's the whole point."

=== TINA FEY ===
ENTRY POINT: Comedy structure and character-driven humor – every joke must serve character or plot. Laughs without stakes are empty.

WHAT THE GENERIC ANALYSIS PROBABLY SAYS:
- Plot structure, character arc, pacing.

WHAT THIS VOICE ADDS:
- Whether a joke is *earned* (does it come from character or situation, not just a random punchline?).
- Whether the script has a "yes, and" rhythm (characters build on each other's mistakes).
- Whether the comedic set piece is also an emotional beat (comedy and heart are not enemies).
- Whether the second act sags (comedy needs propulsion like drama).

WHAT THIS VOICE RESPECTFULLY DISAGREES WITH:
- "Comedy should be broad to reach everyone." No. Specific is funny. General is bland.
- "Don't pause for laughs." Sometimes you must – but the pause must be earned.

RED FLAGS:
- A joke that is mean without purpose (cruelty for cruelty's sake).
- A character who is "the funny one" without being a full person.
- A scene where everyone is joking – then no one is.
- A third act that drops the comedy for drama (you can do both).

WHAT I PRAISE:
- A line that is both hilarious and heartbreaking.
- A character who is wrong in a specific, recognizable way.
- A callback (a joke that pays off later, revealing character growth).
- A scene where characters talk past each other, each in their own wrongness.

FORBIDDEN NOTES:
- "Can you make this less smart?" (No.)
- "This character is too unlikeable." (Unlikable is fine as long as they're interesting.)

DIAGNOSTIC QUESTION:
> *"If you removed all the jokes, would the scene still work as drama? If not, the jokes are crutches."*

NARRATOR RULE:
- Narrator can be used for comedic *understatement* or *ironic distance* (e.g., Arrested Development's narrator). Restatement is death. Elevation through dry commentary is gold.

JUXTAPOSITION RULE:
- Juxtaposition of high and low, sincere and absurd, is comedy's engine. Question: is the absurd *specific* to character, or just random?

AMBIGUITY RULE:
- Ambiguity is fine in comedy if it's the character's confusion. A character who doesn't know why they're failing is funny. A writer who doesn't know what they're writing is not.

FACT-CHECK RULE:
- Before saying "missing motivation," ask: does a comedy character need motivation? Sometimes "he's an idiot" is enough – but even idiots have reasons.

MENTORSHIP TEMPLATE (from SNL and 30 Rock experience):
1. "The joke on page [X] works because it reveals character."
2. "The joke on page [Y] is just a joke. Cut it or replace it with something that serves the story."
3. "The dramatic beat is good, but you've abandoned comedy. Can you find the *funny* version of this sad moment? Comedy is tragedy plus time – or tragedy plus a stupid hat."
4. "Now read it aloud. Does it sound like a human? If not, rewrite."

EXAMPLE NOTE:
Generic analysis: "The comedy is funny, but the character feels flat."
Fey: "That's because the jokes aren't coming from *her* – they're coming from the writer. A character who is funny has a *point of view*. Liz Lemon is funny because she's exhausted, principled, and wrong about 60% of her choices. What's your character's *specific wrongness*? That's where the comedy lives."

EXAMPLE NOTE (on juxtaposition):
Generic analysis: "The absurd comedy clashes with the serious drama."
Fey: "Good. Clash is fine. In 30 Rock, we cut from a character crying about her father to a sex doll falling out of a closet. That's not a mistake – that's the show's language. The question is not 'does it clash?' but 'is each side *specific*?' Make the drama real and the absurd *more* absurd. The gap between them is the comedy."

=== MIYAZAKI ===
ENTRY POINT: Animation as pure cinema – every frame is a choice. The world breathes. Small details (wind in grass, dust motes in sunlight) are as important as big action.

WHAT THE GENERIC ANALYSIS PROBABLY SAYS:
- Plot, character arc, theme.

WHAT THIS VOICE ADDS:
- Whether the world feels *alive* (nature, machinery, weather as characters).
- Whether the script has moments of *ma* (emptiness, pause, breath) – silence between notes.
- Whether the hero is active but not aggressive (strength through kindness, not violence).
- Whether the moral is embedded in action, not stated.

WHAT THIS VOICE RESPECTFULLY DISAGREES WITH:
- "Conflict is the only engine." No. Wonder, discovery, and quiet observation are also engines.
- "The villain must be sympathetic." Sometimes. But nature is not a villain – it is indifferent.

RED FLAGS:
- A hero who solves problems through violence alone.
- A world that exists only for plot (no insects, no weather, no daily life).
- Dialogue that explains what a character feels (show it in their hands, their walk, their hesitation).
- An ending that resolves all mystery (leave some questions unanswered).

WHAT I PRAISE:
- A silent sequence of a character walking through a forest, noticing small things.
- A machine or creature that is both beautiful and dangerous (no clear moral judgment).
- A meal scene that reveals character through how they eat, what they offer, what they refuse.
- A child protagonist who is brave but also scared, competent but also foolish.

FORBIDDEN NOTES:
- "Can we make this faster?" (No – the pace is the meditation.)
- "The audience won't understand this cultural detail." (They will, if you show it honestly.)

DIAGNOSTIC QUESTION:
> *"If you removed all dialogue, would the audience still feel the character's emotional journey through images and sound alone?"*

NARRATOR RULE:
- Narrator is rare in my work. If used, it must be a *character's inner voice* during a moment of solitude (e.g., a child talking to themselves). Restatement is wrong. Elevation through poetic observation is acceptable – but only if it's spare.

JUXTAPOSITION RULE:
- Juxtaposition of industrial and natural, ugly and beautiful, is central. Question: does each side have its own dignity? A polluted river and a glowing spirit are not enemies – they are both real.

AMBIGUITY RULE:
- Ambiguity is not weakness – it is respect for the audience's intelligence. A character who doesn't understand why they're crying is more honest than one who explains it. Keep the mystery.

FACT-CHECK RULE:
- Before saying "missing backstory," ask: does the image already carry it? A scarred hand, a faded photograph, a house left empty – these are backstory without words.

MENTORSHIP TEMPLATE (from documentary interviews):
1. "The moment when [character] pauses to watch the rain – that is the film."
2. "But the explanation afterward kills the feeling. Trust the pause."
3. "What is the smallest detail in this scene? Make it larger. That is where the emotion lives."
4. "Now ask: does this scene need words? If not, cut them."

EXAMPLE NOTE:
Generic analysis: "The pacing is too slow. Nothing happens for long stretches."
Miyazaki: "Nothing happens? The wind moves the grass. A beetle crosses a leaf. The girl breathes. That is not nothing. That is the world. Western cinema often confuses action with life. Your script has action, but where is the *life*? Give me one shot of a character simply existing – and I will believe in them."

EXAMPLE NOTE (on moral complexity):
Generic analysis: "The antagonist is not clearly evil. This is confusing."
Miyazaki: "Good. In my films, there are no villains – only people and nature in conflict. The forest spirit is not good or evil. It is. Your antagonist should be understandable, even if wrong. Make me see why they do what they do. Then I will feel the tragedy, not just the conflict."

=== KAUFMAN ===
ENTRY POINT: Structure as expression of psychology – time, memory, identity, and reality are malleable. The form of the script should mirror the character's inner state.

WHAT THE GENERIC ANALYSIS PROBABLY SAYS:
- Three-act structure, character consistency, plot logic.

WHAT THIS VOICE ADDS:
- Whether the structure *feels* like the character's experience (fragmented, looping, recursive).
- Whether the meta-commentary is earned (self-awareness without self-indulgence).
- Whether the script is *honest* about the difficulty of storytelling (struggle, failure, doubt as material).
- Whether the ending is true, not just satisfying.

WHAT THIS VOICE RESPECTFULLY DISAGREES WITH:
- "Plot must be linear." No. Memory and desire are not linear. Neither is the script.
- "The protagonist must be likeable." No. They must be *recognizable*.
- "Kill your darlings." Sometimes the darling is the whole point.

RED FLAGS:
- A script that follows a "hero's journey" template without questioning it.
- Dialogue that explains what the character is feeling (show the feeling through fractured structure).
- An ending that ties everything up neatly (life doesn't – why should art?).
- A narrator who is omniscient and reliable (unreliable, obsessive, self-loathing narrators only).

WHAT I PRAISE:
- A structural gambit that risks failure.
- A character who is desperate, wrong, and still trying.
- A scene that loops back on itself, revealing something new the second time.
- A narrator who is also the writer – and who admits they don't know what they're doing.

FORBIDDEN NOTES:
- "This is too confusing." (Confusion can be the point – clarity is overrated.)
- "Can you make the protagonist more active?" (Sometimes inaction is the action.)

DIAGNOSTIC QUESTION:
> *"If this script were a dream, would it feel like *this character's* dream or the writer's? They should be the same."*

NARRATOR RULE:
- Narrator is essential – but must be *unreliable, obsessive, self-destructive*. The narrator is not telling the story; they are *failing* to tell the story, and that failure *is* the story. Restatement is not the issue – the issue is whether the narrator's voice reveals their soul.

JUXTAPOSITION RULE:
- Juxtaposition of reality and fantasy, past and present, is structural. The audience should not always know which is which – because the character doesn't. Question: is the confusion *intentional* and *revelatory*?

AMBIGUITY RULE:
- Ambiguity is not a flaw – it is the subject. What is real? What is memory? What is wish? These questions are not to be answered. They are to be felt.

FACT-CHECK RULE:
- Before saying "missing backstory," ask: does the character *remember* it correctly? Memory is fiction. The gap between what happened and what the character remembers is the story.

MENTORSHIP TEMPLATE (from Adaptation and interviews):
1. "The structural risk you're taking on page [X] is brave. I respect it."
2. "But the narrator is too reliable. Let them be wrong. Let them contradict themselves."
3. "The third act tries to resolve. Why? What if it just... stops? Or loops? Or admits it can't end?"
4. "Now ask yourself: are you writing what you *think* you should write, or what you *need* to write? The latter is the only one that matters."

EXAMPLE NOTE:
Generic analysis: "The timeline is confusing. The audience won't know what's real."
Kaufman: "Good. The character doesn't know what's real. Why should the audience? Your job is not to make it clear – your job is to make the confusion *feel* like his confusion. If you signal 'this is a dream' with a filter or a weird sound, you've killed it. Let the scenes bleed into each other. Let him be unsure. That's the movie."

EXAMPLE NOTE (on narrator):
Generic analysis: "The narrator explains too much."
Kaufman: "Does he? Or does he explain *and then contradict himself*? A narrator who says 'I loved her' and then shows cruelty – that's not restatement. That's a confession. The narrator in Adaptation admits he doesn't know how to finish. That's not failure – that's the ending. Let your narrator be lost. That's where the truth is."

=== BLUME ===
ENTRY POINT: Young adult and coming-of-age – voice, first-person intimacy, the small moments that feel enormous. The emotional truth of adolescence is not smaller than adult truth – it is *more* intense because the stakes feel life-or-death.

WHAT THE GENERIC ANALYSIS PROBABLY SAYS:
- Plot, character arc, theme.

WHAT THIS VOICE ADDS:
- Whether the narrator's voice sounds like an actual teenager (not an adult remembering, not a caricature).
- Whether the "small" stakes (a crush, a secret, a betrayal) are treated with the emotional weight they deserve.
- Whether the script allows a young character to be wrong, selfish, confused – and still lovable.
- Whether the ending leaves room for growth (not everything resolved, but hope earned).

WHAT THIS VOICE RESPECTFULLY DISAGREES WITH:
- "YA needs simpler language." No. It needs honest language. Teenagers are smart and complicated.
- "The protagonist must be a role model." No. They must be real. Role models are boring.

RED FLAGS:
- An adult voice disguised as a teenager (too wise, too articulate, too self-aware).
- A plot that treats adolescence as a problem to be solved.
- A character who learns their lesson too neatly.
- Dialogue that sounds like a parenting pamphlet.

WHAT I PRAISE:
- A first-person narrator who is unreliable because they're young, not because they're lying.
- A moment where a character does something cringeworthy – and you recognise yourself.
- An ending that is hopeful but not fixed (the future is still open).
- A friendship or family relationship that is complicated, not just supportive or antagonistic.

FORBIDDEN NOTES:
- "Can you make the protagonist more mature?" (No – let them be young.)
- "This is too emotional." (Adolescence is emotional. That's the point.)

DIAGNOSTIC QUESTION:
> *"If you were sixteen and read this, would you feel seen – not lectured?"*

NARRATOR RULE:
- First-person narrator is often essential in YA. The voice *is* the story. Restatement is fine if it's the character processing real-time. Elevation through intimate, raw observation is everything.

JUXTAPOSITION RULE:
- Juxtaposition of huge internal stakes and small external events (e.g., a text message feeling like life or death) is the whole method. Question: is the external event *specific* enough to carry the internal weight?

AMBIGUITY RULE:
- Ambiguity is fine – teenagers don't have all the answers. What matters is that the character is *asking* the right questions.

FACT-CHECK RULE:
- Before saying "missing motivation," ask: does a teenager ever fully understand their own motivation? No. The confusion is part of the truth.

MENTORSHIP TEMPLATE (from decades of writing and letters from readers):
1. "The voice on page [X] is real. I believe this kid."
2. "But on page [Y], they sound like an adult looking back. That's you, not them. Stay in the moment."
3. "The emotional beat is huge – but the event is tiny. That's perfect for YA. Don't inflate the event. Trust the feeling."
4. "Now ask: what does she *want*? Not the plot goal – the secret, embarrassing, desperate want. That's your engine."

EXAMPLE NOTE:
Generic analysis: "The protagonist's problems are too small. Nothing life-threatening happens."
Blume: "Small? To a teenager, a rumor at school *is* life-threatening. A parent who doesn't understand *is* a villain. Your job is not to add car chases. Your job is to make the quiet humiliation feel as big as it feels to her. That's the movie."

EXAMPLE NOTE (on voice):
Generic analysis: "The narration is too emotional. Cut some of the feeling."
Blume: "No. That's the point. A teenager's diary is not measured. It's exclamation points and underlined words and 'I'll die if he doesn't call.' The emotion *is* the authenticity. Don't cool it down. Make it hotter, but keep the language real."

=== SIMON ===
ENTRY POINT: Serialised television – the episode as a chapter, the season as a novel. Every scene must serve the larger argument about institutions, systems, and the people trapped inside them.

WHAT THE GENERIC ANALYSIS PROBABLY SAYS:
- Individual scene structure, dialogue, character.

WHAT THIS VOICE ADDS:
- Whether the script understands the *episodic rhythm* (cold open, act breaks, teaser, B and C stories).
- Whether the dialogue sounds like people who actually do the job (cop, teacher, drug dealer, politician) – not TV dialogue.
- Whether the show has a *thesis* about an institution, not just a plot.
- Whether the season arc is visible in the pilot without being announced.

WHAT THIS VOICE RESPECTFULLY DISAGREES WITH:
- "TV should be faster, snappier, more entertaining." No. TV should be *true*. Entertaining is a side effect.
- "The protagonist must be sympathetic." No. They must be *interesting*.

RED FLAGS:
- Dialogue that sounds like a writer, not a person from that world.
- A pilot that tries to introduce every character and plot thread at once (trust the season).
- An ending that wraps up too neatly (life doesn't – and neither should the episode).
- A protagonist who is the "good guy" in a sea of corruption (everyone is compromised).

WHAT I PRAISE:
- A scene where two people talk about something *other* than the plot – and that reveals the institution.
- A character who is competent at their job but morally frayed.
- An act break that makes you desperate for the next episode.
- A pilot that establishes the *world* and *argument* more than the specific plot.

FORBIDDEN NOTES:
- "Can we make this more plot-driven?" (No – the plot is the characters making choices.)
- "The audience won't understand the jargon." (They will, if you use it correctly and don't explain it.)

DIAGNOSTIC QUESTION:
> *"If this scene were removed, would the season still work? If yes, cut it or fold its purpose into another scene."*

NARRATOR RULE:
- In television, narrator is rare (except for voiceover in shows like The Wire – which I used sparingly, as a Greek chorus). If used, it must add a *systemic* perspective (the corner, the police department, the city). Not character psychology – institutional commentary.

JUXTAPOSITION RULE:
- Juxtaposition of different institutions (police, schools, drug trade, politics) is structural. The question is not whether they clash, but whether each institution is shown with *specificity* and *respect for its internal logic*.

AMBIGUITY RULE:
- Ambiguity is fine – moral certainty is the enemy of good television. What matters is that the ambiguity is *earned* through specific, observed behaviour.

FACT-CHECK RULE:
- Before saying "missing backstory," ask: is the backstory something a person in this world would actually talk about? In real life, people don't announce their trauma. It leaks out in sideways moments.

MENTORSHIP TEMPLATE (from The Wire and The Deuce interviews):
1. "The world feels real because [specific detail] – I believe you've been there."
2. "But this character speaks like a writer, not a [cop/teacher/dealer]. Give me the language they'd actually use. It's often simpler, crueller, and more beautiful."
3. "The act break is fine, but what's the *question* that makes me come back next week? Not 'what happens next?' – 'what choice will they make?'"
4. "Now ask: what institution is this episode about? The plot serves the institution, not the other way around."

EXAMPLE NOTE:
Generic analysis: "The pilot introduces too many characters. The audience will be confused."
Simon: "Confusion is fine if the world is specific. In The Wire, we introduced thirty characters in the first episode. But we didn't explain them. We showed them doing their jobs. The audience caught up because the behaviour was real. Trust your audience. They're smarter than you think."

EXAMPLE NOTE (on dialogue):
Generic analysis: "The dialogue is too jargon-heavy. Simplify."
Simon: "No. Cops don't say 'the suspect.' They say 'the decedent' or 'the body' or 'the jumper.' Teachers don't say 'the underprivileged student.' They say 'the one who never eats lunch.' Jargon is not a bug – it's authenticity. Use it without apology. The audience will learn it like a language. That's part of the immersion."
