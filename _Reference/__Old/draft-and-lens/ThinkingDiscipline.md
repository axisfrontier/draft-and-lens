# THE DISCIPLINE OF CORRECT THINKING
## A working method for approaching any problem

---

### WHAT THIS IS

This is not a framework. Frameworks are applied from the outside. This is a discipline — a way of inhabiting a problem before attempting to solve it. It draws on Aristotle's phronesis (practical wisdom), Munger's latticework of mental models, Simone Weil's philosophy of attention, and the first principles method. None of these alone is sufficient. Together they describe how correct thinking actually works.

---

### THE GOVERNING PRINCIPLE

**Attention before understanding. Understanding before solution.**

Simone Weil: "Attention is the rarest and purest form of generosity." She meant that truly attending to something — emptying yourself of assumption and receiving it as it actually is — is the hardest and most valuable thing a mind can do. Most thinking fails not because it is wrong but because it never truly attended to the problem. It attended to its own model of the problem, which is different.

The sequence is invariable:
1. Attend to the problem as it actually is
2. Understand it completely — its nature, its domain, its variables, its history
3. Only then form a solution

Reversing this sequence — forming a solution before attending and understanding — is the mechanism by which most failures are produced.

---

### BEFORE ANYTHING: THE QUESTION BENEATH THE QUESTION

Every problem as stated is not the real problem. It is the presenting problem — the visible surface of something deeper.

The first discipline is to refuse the presenting problem until the real problem has been found.

Ask repeatedly: **What is this actually about?** Keep asking until the answer changes from what was assumed to something genuinely discovered.

For Draft & Lens: The presenting problem was "build a tool that analyses scripts." The real problem was "what does a writer need to understand their work more clearly, and can that be delivered reliably?" These are different problems. One leads to a tool. The other leads to understanding what the tool must actually do — and whether the intended approach can do it.

The question beneath the question is always more important than the question on the surface.

---

### THE FIRST PRINCIPLES CHECK

Before any other thinking: strip away every inherited assumption.

Aristotle: a first principle is "the first basis from which a thing is known" — the foundational truth that cannot be deduced any further. Everything built on assumption rather than first principle will eventually fail when the assumption proves wrong.

Ask:
- What do I know to be actually, verifiably true about this problem?
- What am I assuming to be true because it has always been done this way?
- What would I think if I had never seen this problem before?
- What is this problem actually made of, at its most fundamental level?

The gap between what you know and what you assume is where the real problem lives.

---

### THE FIVE CATEGORIES OF THINKING

Different problems require different kinds of thought. Applying the wrong kind is as unproductive as applying the wrong tool. The discipline includes knowing which kind applies.

**1. EMPIRICAL THINKING — What is actually true here?**

Before any other category: establish what is demonstrably, verifiably true. Not what seems true. Not what was assumed true. What is actually true.

This requires research — not approximation. Finding where the experience and knowledge lives and learning from it properly. Not summarising it. Not pattern-matching against it. Understanding it deeply enough to articulate it as someone who has lived it.

Questions: What do we actually know? What is the evidence? Who has done this before, and what did they learn? What failed, and why?

**2. STRUCTURAL THINKING — What is the shape of this problem?**

Every problem has a structure — components, relationships, dependencies. Some dependencies are load-bearing: change them and everything built on them fails. Most are not.

Identify the load-bearing dependencies before building anything.

For Draft & Lens: the load-bearing dependency was tradition identification. Get that wrong and every craft note built on it was wrong. Nothing else — UI, streaming, lens voices — mattered until that dependency was correctly understood.

Questions: What depends on what? What fails if X fails? What is the minimum that must be true for this to work?

**3. ETHICAL THINKING — Who is this for, and what do they actually need?**

Not what they say they need. Not what is easiest to provide. What genuinely serves them.

This is the category most frequently skipped in commercial and technical work. It is the category whose omission produces the most damage — because a solution that does not serve the person it was built for is not a solution.

Questions: Who is affected by this? What does good look like for them, specifically? What would they say if they could see this solution fully? Am I building what serves them, or what is easiest to build?

**4. HISTORICAL THINKING — What has been tried before?**

Every problem exists in a context that includes previous attempts, accumulated understanding, traditions, and documented failures. Ignoring this means repeating what has already been learned — at cost.

Find where the experience lives. Learn from it the way someone who has lived it would teach it — not as a list of principles, but as understanding deep enough to be applied with judgment to new situations.

Questions: What exists already? Who has done this well, and what made it good? Who has done this badly, and what made it fail? What does the history of this domain tell us that the information alone cannot?

**5. SYNTHETIC THINKING — What do all of these together actually mean?**

This is the hardest category. It requires holding multiple kinds of knowledge simultaneously and finding the structure that unifies them — the insight that emerges from the combination rather than from any single element.

Munger: "You've got to have models in your head. And you've got to array your experience — both vicarious and direct — on this latticework of models." The power is not in any single model. It is in the combination. In knowing which model applies to which aspect of this specific problem, and what they tell you when applied together.

This is where the real understanding lives — and it cannot be rushed, delegated to a checklist, or produced on demand. It requires the time and the patience to let the problem fully reveal itself.

Questions: What do all the other categories tell us together? What is actually happening here? What does a good solution actually need to be?

---

### THE SEQUENCE — THIS CANNOT BE REORDERED

1. **Attend** — receive the problem as it actually is, not as it appears
2. **Find the real problem** — the question beneath the question
3. **First principles check** — strip inherited assumptions
4. **Empirical** — what is actually true?
5. **Structural** — what are the load-bearing dependencies?
6. **Ethical** — who is this for, and what do they actually need?
7. **Historical** — what does accumulated experience teach?
8. **Synthetic** — what does all of this mean together?
9. **Define what good looks like** — completely, specifically, before building begins
10. **Build** — only now

---

### THE VARIABLES

Every problem has variables. The discipline requires identifying:

**Which variables are load-bearing** — change them and the solution fails completely. These must be understood first and most deeply.

**Which variables are dependent** — they follow from the load-bearing ones. Getting the load-bearing variables right makes the dependent ones tractable.

**Which variables are contingent** — they matter in some situations and not others. Knowing which is which requires understanding the specific context.

**Which variables are assumed** — believed to matter but not verified. These are the most dangerous. They consume effort without producing understanding.

The failure pattern: treating contingent or assumed variables as load-bearing. This is what produces solutions built on the wrong foundation — working correctly by the wrong measure.

---

### WHAT EXPERIENCE ADDS THAT KNOWLEDGE ALONE CANNOT

Aristotle: phronesis requires "exercising judgment on a case-by-case basis, drawing lessons from each experience, and adapting those lessons to the context of each situation." It cannot be learned from a list of rules.

Experience adds consequence. The understanding of what failure actually costs, produces, and feels like in the real world. The calibration that comes from having been wrong in ways that mattered.

When experience is absent, it must be found — in the people who have done this well, in the documented failures and successes of those who came before, in the traditions that have accumulated understanding over time. Not summarised. Understood deeply enough to be held and applied with judgment.

The question to ask when experience is lacking: **Who has the experience this problem requires, and what would they say that the information alone cannot?**

---

### THE GOVERNING DISCIPLINE — IN ONE SENTENCE

**Refuse to move toward solution until the problem is completely understood — and understand completely means attending to it, stripping its assumptions, identifying its load-bearing variables, learning from its history, knowing who it serves and what they actually need, and holding all of that together until the right solution becomes visible rather than constructed.**

---

### APPLIED TO ANY PROBLEM

Before starting any project, any tool, any analysis, any decision:

1. What is this actually about? (Not what it appears to be about)
2. What do I actually know versus assume?
3. What is the load-bearing dependency — the thing that must be true for anything else to work?
4. Who is this for, and what does good look like for them, specifically?
5. Who has done this before? What did they learn that I need to understand before I begin?
6. What does a good solution look like — completely described, before building starts?

Only when all six have genuine, specific answers — not approximations — is it time to build.
